I share the view presented in this thesis.

 

Essentially, IOM enlarged dramatically in a short period of time (staff, budget, member states, geographical presence, diversification of programatic activities). The current size of Organization with 10,000+ staff and close to 400 offices demanded professional transformation of management and organizational structure, which did not occur. Instead of creating and empowering through proper delegation an amalgamation of professional managers (external labor force) and internal experience, the transformation was entrusted to internal force, who were experienced in their own technical domains, but not being trained for complexity of big systems.

 

Additional transformational challenge is related to joining the UN system. While projectization is a funding model perfectly suited for "previous IOM" (Agency service oriented), the new UN Migration agency has additional agenda which is not quite "service oriented".

The question  s now what GSAh can do, or should do?

 

While recognizing that the GSAC's role/mandate is not to take a part in managing the Organization, I believe that both staff and Administration should have the same goal of making the Organization the best it can be. With that in mind, GSAC should transmit staff views on management impact and issues. After all, poor management have impact on well-being of GSA members.

 

My position is that GSAC reservations vis-a-vis management directions should be kept internally.
